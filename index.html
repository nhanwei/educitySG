The Singapore Education Story

Hi, I am Sharifah (the one with mouth open)

Borned in 1940s, this is my EduStory

*Sharifah is the personification of Singapore's pioneer generation based on our conversations with family and friends.

What were classrooms like in the good old days?

Well, I had no modern luxury of iPads, laptops, smartphones to aid/distract my studies. On the other hand, I might had gotten away with more than my fair share of mischief as each teacher had to take care of many more students.

Mouse over the graph for more details. [Note: These are not number of students in each classroom but a simple division of number of students by number of teachers].

Despite doing reasonably well in school, I faced much difficulties in advancing my education

With many mouths to feed, my family had troubles supporting my post secondary education. In fact, in the early 1990s, less than 50% of my friends had attended post secondary eduction. But I was determined to further my studies.

I finally made it into university, become one of the first among my friends to receive tertiary education

This was the 1990s and I was lucky as it seemed like many families then preferred to support their sons for tertiary education. The good news is, the gender gap had closed over the years and even reversed in trend in 2007, where there were more female students than male tertiary students.

More funding. More competitive.

Singapore's government has invested heavily in education over the years. Although my children may have more opportunities for education than I ever had, a university degree is no longer the golden ticket to job security and career success. My children will need to work as hard as me to build their own EduStory.

The Public Service Commission (PSC) Scholarship

Created in 1961, PSC has been a source of talent pool for key leaders in the Singapore Government. Here, we delve deeper into the numbers behind the scholarship system.



Here, every scholar from 2002 to 2013 is represented by a bubble. Feel free to group them by clicking the buttons below.

*257 scholars had no gender data, so we tagged half of them as males, the other half as females.

Decline in proportion of STEM scholars

It seemed that the proportion of Science, Technology, Engineering, Math (STEM) scholars has decreased over the years; they seemingly are replaced by humanities scholars. With the growing importance of technology, will we see an resurgence of STEM scholars? Or do brilliant young STEMsters prefer to join large, famous technology MNCs?

Number of scholars per year dipped to a low point in 2005 but has been increasing since

The trend is very interesting as there seemed to be no clear indication what caused the low point in scholarship numbers in 2005 and its subsequent rise. Perhaps it depended on the demand of talents in the public office.

Decline in proportion of engineering scholars

By breaking up the scholar's education mix, we can see that fewer and fewer scholars chose to study engineering (blue), instead, more and more are opting for economics (orange), humanities (green) and law (red).


Ultimately, is this a worrying trend? Or does the degree not matter since most scholars will be involved in planning and policy work? Perhaps only time will tell but it does seem like Singapore government is interested to increase the number of STEM scholars.
